# Hook Frameworks

Structured approaches to creating hooks. Each framework has a distinct pattern and use case. When generating hooks, aim for 2 variations per framework.

---

## Frameworks

### 1. Question Hook
Opens with a provocative or relatable question that the viewer wants answered.

**Pattern**: "[Question that implies valuable answer]?"

**When to use**:
- Addressing common pain points
- Challenging assumptions
- Problem-aware audiences

### 2. Story/Scene Hook
Drops the viewer into a specific moment or scenario. Creates immediate immersion.

**Pattern**: "I was [specific situation] when [unexpected thing happened]..."

- Personal testimonials
- Discovery narratives

### 3. Contrarian Hook
Challenges conventional wisdom or popular belief. Creates tension that demands resolution.

**Pattern**: "[Common belief] is wrong. Here's why..." or "Stop [common advice]."

**When to use**:
- Differentiation from competitors
- Sophisticated audiences
- When you have genuine insight

### 5. Pain Point Hook
Names the problem directly and viscerally. Makes the viewer feel seen.

**Pattern**: "If you [specific frustration], this is for you."

**When to use**:

**Pattern**: "[Number] people did [thing] and [result]..." or "Everyone keeps asking me about..."

## Framework Selection by Awareness Level

| Awareness Level | Best Frameworks |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Unaware | Stat/Fact, Pattern Interrupt, Story |
| Problem Aware | Pain Point, Question, Contrarian |
| Solution Aware | Social Proof, Before/After |
| Product Aware | Social Proof, Contrarian |
| Most Aware | Before/After, Story (deeper) |
